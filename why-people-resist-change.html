Why People Resist Change — And What Data Tells Us About It

Drawing on behavioral psychology and my experience leading change management at a global bank, I explore the science behind resistance and what actually moves people.

JULY 2026 · SAVANNA WARE

A few years into leading change management at a global bank, I stopped asking "why won't people just get on board?" It's the wrong question. The better one is: what is actually happening in someone's brain and their calculation of risk when a new system, process, or reporting line lands on their desk? Once I started treating resistance as data rather than defiance, the work got a lot more effective — and a lot less personal.

Resistance is a feature of the brain, not a character flaw

The instinct in most organizations is to read resistance as stubbornness, laziness, or a lack of vision. Neuroscience tells a different story. The amygdala, the brain's threat-detection center, doesn't distinguish well between a physical danger and a social one. A restructuring announcement, an unfamiliar workflow, or a new manager can trigger the same fight-flight-freeze response as an actual threat, flooding the body with cortisol and pulling resources away from the prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for reasoned, flexible thinking. That's why perfectly intelligent, capable people sometimes respond to a rollout email with what looks like irrational panic. It isn't irrational. It's biology. Uncertainty itself is often enough to trigger this response, even before anyone knows whether the change is good or bad for them.

I saw this constantly in banking, where change usually arrives wrapped in acronyms and mandates from a regulator or a head office nobody in the room has met. The less context people had, the more threat their brains manufactured to fill the gap.

Losses loom larger than gains

Behavioral economists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky gave us the concept of loss aversion: the pain of losing something is felt roughly twice as intensely as the pleasure of gaining something of equal value. Applied to change, this means employees don't weigh a new system against its promised benefits — they weigh it against everything they stand to lose: fluency, status, relationships, control over their own time. Status quo bias follows naturally from this. If the current state feels safe and familiar, any change reads as a potential loss before it reads as an opportunity, regardless of how good the change actually is on paper.

This is why the business case that excites a steering committee often falls flat on the front line. Leadership sees the upside; the team closest to the work sees what's at risk. Both are responding rationally to the same information, just from different vantage points.

The evidence on what actually works

The good news is this isn't just diagnosis — there's a real evidence base on what reduces resistance.

The clearest signal comes from one of the oldest studies in organizational psychology. In 1948, Lester Coch and John French studied resistance to job changes at a Virginia textile plant and found that groups given a real role in planning the change adapted faster and grumbled less than groups who were simply told what to do. Later researchers pushed back on some of the original interpretation — training, clearer explanations, and fair pay likely did some of the work too — but the core finding has held up across decades of replication: involvement changes the psychological experience of change, not just its logistics.

More recent research on organizational justice tells a similar story. Employees are far more likely to accept a change when they see the process behind it as fair, even if they don't like the outcome. One study found people were roughly 43% less likely to push back on a decision when they were given an adequate explanation for it, independent of whether the decision itself favored them. In other words, people aren't just protesting the change — they're often protesting being left out of how it happened to them.

Prosci's research across thousands of change initiatives points the same direction. Projects with strong change management practices meet or exceed their objectives about 88% of the time, compared to roughly 13% where change management is weak. Active, visible sponsorship from senior leaders alone lifts the odds of success by around 29%, and organizations with strong sponsors succeed at nearly three times the rate of those with weak ones. None of this is about better slide decks. It's about whether the people affected by a decision see themselves reflected in how it was made and communicated.

A word on the "70% fail" statistic

You've probably heard that 70% of change initiatives fail. I used to cite it too, until I looked for where it came from. It traces back to an offhand, explicitly "unscientific estimate" in a 1993 business book, repeated without a source in a 2000 Harvard Business Review article, and cited by nearly everyone since — including consulting firms with an obvious interest in the number sounding grim. There's no rigorous study behind it. That doesn't mean change is easy; plenty of initiatives do underperform. But it's worth being honest about which numbers are real and which ones just feel true because failed change is what sticks in memory. Overstating the odds of failure can itself become a self-fulfilling script that makes people more defensive going in.

What this looked like in practice

At the bank, the shift that mattered most wasn't a new framework — it was sequencing. We stopped announcing decisions and started testing them with the people who'd have to live with them, even in small, informal ways, before anything went firmwide. We got explicit about naming the loss, not just the gain: acknowledging directly that a new process would mean giving up a workflow someone had spent years mastering, rather than pretending the change was purely additive. And we treated over-explaining as a feature, not a waste of time, because ambiguity is what the amygdala fears most.

None of this eliminates resistance, and it shouldn't. Some resistance is useful signal — a sign that a plan has a real gap or that a team knows something leadership doesn't. The goal was never to engineer compliance. It was to give people's brains enough safety, fairness, and information that they could actually engage with the substance of the change, instead of defending against the threat of it.
