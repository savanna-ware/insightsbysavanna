Nearly Five Years in France: What Living Abroad Taught Me About Business

From Rhode Island to Paris — how navigating a foreign culture, language, and work environment sharpened my analytical thinking and communication skills.

JULY 2026 · SAVANNA WARE

I moved from Rhode Island to Paris without a clean plan for how long I'd stay. Almost five years later, I'm still here — now working as a business analyst at Société Générale and finishing a master's in data science and business analysis. Somewhere in those years, the move stopped being an adventure I was having and became a skill set I use every day at work, one I don't think I could have built any other way.

The research backs up what it feels like

There's a reasonable body of research on why living abroad reshapes how people think, not just what they know. Studies on multicultural experience consistently find a link between time spent navigating a foreign culture and creative, flexible problem-solving — the kind of thinking that lets you generate more than one plausible answer to a question instead of defaulting to the first familiar one. One line of research traces this to cognitive flexibility and openness to experience, which together account for a substantial share of the gap in creative performance between people with extensive cross-cultural exposure and those without it.

The mechanism makes intuitive sense once you've lived it. Constantly comparing "how it's done here" to "how it was done at home" forces you to hold two systems in your head at once and evaluate both, rather than assuming there's only one correct way to run a meeting, structure a project, or deliver feedback. You lose the reflex of treating your default as the default.

Learning to hear what wasn't said

The clearest professional lesson came from communication style, not language. Researcher Erin Meyer's Culture Map places countries on a scale from low-context communication, where good communication is explicit and literal, to high-context communication, where a lot of the meaning lives between the lines. The U.S. sits firmly on the low-context end; France sits well toward high-context. Early on, that gap cost me. I'd leave meetings confident I understood the outcome, only to find out later that the real decision had been communicated in a tone, a pause, or a comment I'd taken at face value instead of reading for subtext.

What made it more disorienting is that the same framework separates communication style from directness of feedback — and France scores as more direct on criticism than the U.S., even while being more indirect on general communication. That combination doesn't exist in American workplace norms, where indirect communication and blunt feedback don't usually travel together. I had to unlearn the assumption that those two things move in the same direction, and rebuild a more accurate model of what any given comment actually meant.

The skill this builds isn't "understanding France." It's learning to treat your own read of a situation as one hypothesis among several, and to actively check it — a habit that turns out to be just as useful in a data review as it is in a Parisian staff meeting.

What it changed about how I work

Three habits followed me directly into analyst work. First, I stopped assuming a single explanation for a number or a decision was the right one just because it was the first one offered — the instinct to hold multiple readings of the same situation, built from years of double-checking my own cultural assumptions, turned into double-checking my own analytical assumptions. Second, I got much more deliberate about how I communicate findings to different audiences, because I'd spent years learning the hard way that the same sentence can land completely differently depending on who's hearing it and what they're listening for. Third, I became comfortable being the least informed person in the room, which is an uncomfortable but genuinely useful place to operate from as an analyst — it's forced me to ask better questions instead of assuming context I don't have.

None of this was the plan when I got on a plane five years ago. But the traits that make someone effective at navigating an unfamiliar culture — tolerance for ambiguity, willingness to be wrong about your first read, patience for the slow work of actually understanding a system before you try to change it — turned out to be the same traits that make someone effective at translating data into decisions other people trust.

Sources: research on multicultural experience, cognitive flexibility, and creativity (Simonton, 2012, meta-analytic findings on cross-cultural experience and creative performance); Erin Meyer's Culture Map framework on high- vs. low-context communication and the Evaluating scale (summary, Erin Meyer).
